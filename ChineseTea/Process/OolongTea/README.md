# Oolong Tea (乌龙茶)

[中文版](README_cn.md) | [← Back to Home](../../../README.md)

## Overview

Oolong tea is the most complex tea category in terms of processing. It is a **partially oxidized** tea, with oxidation levels ranging from as low as 10% to as high as 70%. This wide range gives oolong tea an extraordinary diversity of flavors — from light and floral to rich and roasted.

## Production Process

**Full process:** Picking → Withering → Shaking/Bruising → Kill-green → Rolling → Drying/Roasting

The key step unique to oolong is **shaking and bruising (摇青/做青)**, where the leaves are gently tossed and their edges are bruised to promote partial oxidation. The tea master controls the degree of oxidation by adjusting the duration and intensity of shaking, making this process highly skill-dependent.

## Major Oolong Tea Regions and Varieties

### Fujian — Northern (闽北)
- **Da Hong Pao (大红袍)** — "Big Red Robe," a heavily roasted Wuyi rock tea (岩茶) with deep mineral and floral notes.
- **Rou Gui (肉桂)** — A Wuyi rock tea known for its cinnamon-like spicy aroma.
- **Shui Xian (水仙)** — Smooth, with orchid-like fragrance.

### Fujian — Southern (闽南)
- **Tieguanyin (铁观音)** — One of China's most famous teas. Light-oxidized versions are floral and fragrant; traditional versions are roasted and mellow.

### Guangdong
- **Fenghuang Dancong (凤凰单丛)** — Known for mimicking natural fragrances: honey orchid, almond, gardenia, and more.

### Taiwan
- **Dong Ding (冻顶)** — Medium-roasted, smooth and sweet.
- **Oriental Beauty (东方美人)** — Heavily oxidized, honey-sweet with a fruity aroma.
- **Ali Shan (阿里山)** — High-mountain oolong, light and floral.

## Brewing Guide

- **Water temperature:** 90–100°C (higher for roasted oolongs)
- **Tea-to-water ratio:** 5–8g per 100ml (gongfu style)
- **Steeping time:** Start with 10–15 seconds, gradually increase
- **Teaware:** Yixing clay teapot or porcelain gaiwan
- **Method:** Gongfu brewing is recommended to fully explore the layers of flavor across multiple infusions.
