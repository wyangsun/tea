## The Rich History of Chinese Tea

Tea traces its roots to ancient China, thousands of years ago. The Chinese were the first to discover the magic of tea leaves, and this discovery has become an integral part of our culture.

### Historical Timeline

| Era | Period | Milestone |
|-----|--------|----------|
| Mythical | ~2737 BC | Emperor Shen Nong discovers tea when leaves blow into his boiling water |
| Han Dynasty | 206 BC – 220 AD | Tea used as medicine and tribute; earliest written records |
| Tang Dynasty | 618–907 | Lu Yu writes *The Classic of Tea* (茶经), the world's first tea monograph |
| Song Dynasty | 960–1279 | Whisked powdered tea (点茶) becomes popular; tea competitions flourish |
| Ming Dynasty | 1368–1644 | Loose-leaf steeping replaces powdered tea; six tea categories begin to emerge |
| Qing Dynasty | 1644–1912 | Tea trade via Silk Road and sea routes; Pu'er and oolong gain prominence |
| Modern | 1900s–present | Chinese tea culture revives; gongfu brewing becomes widespread |

## The Six Major Tea Categories

China boasts a remarkable variety of teas, each with its own unique character and flavor profile. The six major categories are classified by their degree of oxidation and processing method:

### Oxidation Spectrum

```
  Unoxidized                              Fully Oxidized    Post-fermented
  ◄─────────────────────────────────────────────────────►   ◄──────────►

  ██░░░░░░░░  Green Tea   绿茶     (Kill-green, no oxidation)
  ██░░░░░░░░  White Tea   白茶     (Natural withering, minimal oxidation)
  ███░░░░░░░  Yellow Tea  黄茶     (Kill-green + sealed yellowing)
  ████████░░  Oolong Tea  乌龙茶   (Partial oxidation, 10–70%)
  ██████████  Black Tea   红茶     (Full oxidation)
                                                            ██████████
                          Dark Tea  黑茶     (Microbial post-fermentation)
```

| Category | Chinese Name | Oxidation Level | Key Characteristic |
|----------|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| [Green Tea](ChineseTea/Process/GreenTea/README.md) | 绿茶 | Unoxidized | Fresh, vegetal flavor |
| [White Tea](ChineseTea/Process/WhiteTea/README.md) | 白茶 | Minimal | Delicate, sweet taste |
| [Yellow Tea](ChineseTea/Process/YellowTea/README.md) | 黄茶 | Slight | Mellow, smooth body |
| [Oolong Tea](ChineseTea/Process/OolongTea/README.md) | 乌龙茶 | Partial (10–70%) | Complex, layered aroma |
| [Black Tea](ChineseTea/Process/BlackTea/README.md) | 红茶 | Fully oxidized | Bold, robust flavor |
| [Dark Tea](ChineseTea/Process/DarkTea/README.md) | 黑茶 | Post-fermented | Aged, earthy charm |

> **Note:** Pu'er tea is a collective term for raw Pu'er (Sheng, similar to green tea in processing — still debated) and ripe Pu'er (Shou, a type of dark tea) from Yunnan province.

### Processing Methods at a Glance

| Tea | Key Steps | Unique Process |
|-----|----------|---------------|
| Green | Pick → **Kill-green** → Roll → Dry | Kill-green (杀青) halts all oxidation |
| White | Pick → **Wither** → Dry | Simplest process; no kill-green |
| Yellow | Pick → Kill-green → Roll → **Sealed Yellowing** → Dry | Sealed yellowing (闷黄) adds mellowness |
| Oolong | Pick → Wither → **Shake/Bruise** → Kill-green → Roll → Dry | Shaking (摇青) controls partial oxidation |
| Black | Pick → Wither → Roll → **Full Oxidation** → Dry | Complete enzymatic oxidation |
| Dark | Pick → Kill-green → Roll → **Piling (渥堆)** → Dry | Microbial fermentation over weeks/months |

## The Art of Brewing Chinese Tea

The traditional Chinese tea ceremony is a true art form — an exquisite choreography of water, leaves, and time. It embodies respect, mindfulness, and appreciation. To brew and enjoy Chinese tea properly, you must consider:

- **Water quality** — Spring water or filtered water is preferred
- **Teaware** — Porcelain gaiwan, Yixing clay teapot, or glass cup depending on tea type
- **Water temperature** — Ranges from 70°C (green tea) to 100°C (dark tea)
- **Steeping time** — Varies by tea type and personal preference

### Quick Brewing Reference

| Tea Type | Temperature | Tea/Water Ratio | Steep Time | Best Teaware |
|----------|------------|----------------|-----------|-------------|
| Green Tea | 75–85°C | 3g / 150ml | 1–3 min | Glass cup |
| White Tea | 85–95°C | 3–5g / 150ml | 1–2 min | Gaiwan |
| Yellow Tea | 80–85°C | 3g / 150ml | 1–2 min | Glass cup |
| Oolong Tea | 90–100°C | 5–8g / 100ml | 10–15 sec | Gaiwan / Yixing pot |
| Black Tea | 85–95°C | 3–5g / 150ml | 2–3 min | Gaiwan / Teapot |
| Dark Tea | 95–100°C | 5–8g / 100ml | 10–15 sec | Yixing pot |

## Health Benefits of Chinese Tea

Beyond its delightful taste, Chinese tea offers a myriad of potential health benefits. Studies suggest that the antioxidants found in tea can contribute to improved well-being. Tea can also be a source of relaxation, a moment of tranquility in our busy lives. It is a beverage that not only delights the palate but also nurtures the body and soul.

## Getting Started

New to Chinese tea? Here are some beginner-friendly recommendations:

| If you prefer... | Try this | Why |
|-----------------|---------|-----|
| Light, refreshing | **Longjing** (Green Tea) | Clean, chestnut-sweet, easy to brew in a glass cup |
| Sweet, gentle | **Fuding Silver Needle** (White Tea) | Naturally sweet, forgiving steep time |
| Rich, aromatic | **Jin Xuan / Milk Oolong** (Oolong Tea) | Creamy, naturally smooth with a subtle milky aroma |
| Bold, warming | **Dianhong** (Black Tea) | Honey-sweet, full-bodied, great with or without milk |
| Deep, earthy | **Ripe Pu'er** (Dark Tea) | Smooth and mellow, excellent after meals |
| Floral, fragrant | **Jasmine Tea** (Flower Tea) | Easy to find, universally enjoyable |

> **Tip:** Start with a gaiwan (盖碗) — a simple lidded bowl that works with all tea types. It's the most versatile piece of Chinese teaware.
